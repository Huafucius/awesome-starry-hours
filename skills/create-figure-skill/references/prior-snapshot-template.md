# Prior Snapshot Template

Read this during **Phase 2 · Prior Capture**. The prior snapshot is the frozen baseline of what the pre-search model believed about this figure; later passes cite it to show what evidence corrected. A snapshot without structure is a puddle — we want a readable substrate.

This is a light skeleton, not a rigid schema. Skip sections that do not apply; add sections the figure demands. The point is that the **same kind of information goes in the same place across figures**, so that downstream agents (and your future self) can compare snapshots and spot systemic blind spots.

## Sections

### 1. Hard-prior beliefs (high confidence)

What you think is widely attested, canonical, redundant in training data. Dates, major works, key events. Be explicit that high confidence is not truth — many "canonical" facts are politically canonized (see `research-stance.md`).

### 2. Thematic / conceptual priors (medium-high confidence)

The principles, metaphors, or concepts the figure is most associated with. Name them plainly. This section often overlaps with what `research/thinking.md` will later rest on.

### 3. Voice or form priors (medium confidence)

What the figure's prose, method, or register feels like from the prior. Include specific lexical / structural signatures if you have them. Mark layer or channel when possible (see `four-axes.md` Axis 3 scope note).

### 4. Relational / biographical priors (mixed confidence)

People in the figure's life — collaborators, rivals, mentors, students, family. Flag where the prior is likely contaminated by hagiography or by a canonical interpretation (e.g., "my Marie Curie prior privileges Pierre-and-Marie partnership; may understate her solo decade").

### 5. Contested / uncomfortable zones

Where the received view is politically or culturally contested. Include the canonical "clean" story AND a note that it is contested. Examples: Nietzsche's sister's forgeries, Curie's Langevin affair, Feynman on women. Do not pre-resolve — flag.

### 6. Suspiciously clean / too-smooth zones

The places your prior feels **too** confident. Training data tends to propagate a single early interpretation. If you can complete a paragraph without any "but" or "although", the paragraph is probably wrong. Name the smoothness explicitly.

### 7. Thin zones (low confidence or no memory)

What the prior does not know. Specific periods, relationships, minor works. Write "thin on X" — do not paper over. These are targets for Phase 3 Evidence Expansion.

### 8. Reception-era self-awareness

**Required question:** "What reception-era or school produced this prior?" For many figures the answer is a single dominant settlement (Kaufmann's Nietzsche; Mao-era 鲁迅; post-*Something Like an Autobiography* Kurosawa). Name it. Without this step, the settlement passes through as "truth".

### 9. Verbatim-confident fragments

Short quotes you hold at verbatim confidence from the prior. List with attribution. Anything longer than ~15 words should be paraphrased unless a primary source has been loaded — Phase 3's job.

### 10. Expected deltas

Where you expect evidence to correct prior. This is speculative on purpose — getting this wrong later is informative. "I expect evidence to show I understated X" or "I suspect my thematic priors are filtered through the Trilling-era read".

## Length

Snapshots usually run 200–500 lines for a substantively known figure. Going much longer is often padding; much shorter usually means skipped honesty work (sections 5, 6, 8 are the commonly skipped ones).

## What a snapshot is not

- Not research. No web search, no external fact-checking, no citation of sources you have not actually loaded.
- Not a Wikipedia summary. The point is the *prior's shape*, including its biases.
- Not revised. Freeze it after Phase 2. Later passes may append dated notes beside it (see `route-planning.md` artifact discipline), but the original body stays.
